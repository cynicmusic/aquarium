# Iteration Review — 2026-04-19

Adversarial critique of Tracks A (fish eyes), B (iridophore layer), and C (cuttlefish geometry). Baseline compared against `aquarium_after_v003.png`, with additional evidence from `cuttle_v001.png` and `cuttle_sheet_001.png`.

---

## Track A: Fish eyes

**Score: 5/10**

### What works
- At normal viewing distance most fish now read as having actual 3D eye geometry rather than the flat decal-style dot visible in the baseline — the hemisphere cornea creates a subtle highlight that helps sell the side profile.
- The glint is small and offset, not plastered dead-center, which is the right instinct — eyes no longer look like googly stickers.
- On the dark/black fish top-right the iridescent ring reads as a proper pupil/iris boundary instead of a painted circle, giving those silhouettes more life than the baseline's uniform white dots.

### What's broken
- Compared to the baseline, I actually see *fewer* crisp eyes in the after shot. The cardinal-red fish center-left, the stingray-ish swimmer mid-frame, and several background fish have eyes that have essentially disappeared into the skin shader — the cornea highlight is being swallowed by the iridophore layer (Track B bleed-through, see below).
- The iridescent ring appears to be a full ring around the cornea rather than a true iris, so on close fish it reads as a bright halo/lens-flare rather than a biological eye. That's a "VFX eye," not a fish eye.
- Glint placement looks world-space rather than view-space on at least one fish — the specular dot is on the *back* side of the eyeball from the camera on the left-facing yellow fish top-left-ish area, which kills the illusion instantly.

### Specific fixes
- Make the iris/ring occupy only the lower ~60% of the cornea hemisphere (mask by local Y in the eye's object space). A full 360 ring always reads as sci-fi, not anatomical.
- Clamp the iridophore shader's influence on eye UVs to zero — whatever mesh/material region flag you're using for skin is leaking onto the eye sockets. If eyes are a separate mesh, confirm they aren't inheriting the skin material; if they're part of the same mesh, add a vertex-color or UV mask channel and gate the thin-film term on it.
- Force the glint to view-space: compute it as `dot(normal, viewDir)` weighted and offset slightly toward the light, not as a fixed world-space lobe. Current look suggests a static emissive patch.
- Scale eye size per-fish by body length — on the tiny background fish the cornea+ring is pixel-for-pixel the same as on the foreground fish, so small fish look bug-eyed.

## Track B: Iridophore layer

**Score: 4/10**

### What works
- The pastel pinks and teals on the foreground rocks/anemone blobs (which presumably share the shader or a sibling) show the thin-film math is functional — you're getting genuine hue shift with view angle, not just a tinted overlay.
- On the large stingray-shape mid-frame the subtle lilac-to-silver shift across the body gives it volume the baseline entirely lacks.
- Default intensity 0.25 was the correct conservative choice — at 0.5+ I'd expect this to be a disaster; at 0.25 it's merely a problem.

### What's broken
- The fish palette has collapsed. Baseline had saturated, distinct reds, oranges, yellows, blues, purples — a coral-reef chromatic spread. The after shot is a muddy lavender/grey/teal wash where half the fish now read as the same desaturated cool-tone. The thin-film layer is acting as a **hue-shift toward cyan/magenta** on top of every base color, compressing variance.
- Pattern visibility is gone. Baseline had visible stripes, spots, gradients on fish bodies (tiger striping on the central fish, the orange-white banding, etc.). In the after shot those patterns are washed out — the thin-film term appears to be additive over the albedo rather than multiplicative-under-Fresnel, which blows out the mid-tones where patterns live.
- The rocks and foreground "egg" blobs are now pink/mauve bubblegum. Baseline had grounded earth-tone rocks that sold the underwater floor. Applying the iridophore layer to non-fish geometry (rocks, plants?) is a category error — iridophores are a fish-skin thing. The scene has lost its sense of a seafloor.

### Specific fixes
- Restrict the iridophore material to fish-body meshes only. Audit the material assignment — I suspect the shader got bound to a shared `StandardMaterial` used by rocks/decor. Add a `isFishSkin: true` userData flag and only apply the thin-film pass to those.
- Change the composite from `albedo + thinFilm * intensity` to `mix(albedo, albedo * thinFilmTint, fresnel * intensity)` so the effect is grazing-angle-only and preserves mid-tone pattern fidelity at face-on angles.
- Drop default intensity from 0.25 to 0.12–0.15 and expose a per-species multiplier — showy reef fish can have 0.3, but dull bottom-dwellers should be ~0.05. A single global value is why the palette is homogenizing.
- Add a chroma-preservation clamp: after the thin-film mix, reassert at least 70% of the original HSV saturation. Right now it desaturates, which is the opposite of what real iridophores do (they *add* chroma, they don't mute it).

## Track C: Cuttlefish geometry

**Score: 3/10**

### What works
- The mantle silhouette in the 4×4 grid is recognizable as a cuttlefish from the side — the pointed rear and rounded anterior read correctly at a glance.
- The arm curl parameter does produce visible variation across the sweep (columns clearly differ from curl 0.10 → 1.00), so the rig is parameterized and the sheet workflow is functioning.
- In `aquarium_after_v003.png` the cuttlefish (mid-frame, the grey/silver creature) integrates into the scene lighting reasonably — it doesn't read as pasted on.

### What's broken
- **No chromatophore shader is visible on the in-scene cuttlefish.** The brief claims a chromatophore shader was added, but the cuttlefish in `aquarium_after_v003.png` is a uniform flat grey — no spots, no pulse, no dynamic pattern. Either the shader isn't wired up, isn't animating, or its intensity is 0. This is the headline feature of the track and it is not shipping.
- The cuttle_v001 preview shows severe anatomy problems: the eyes are two giant wooden spheres sitting *on top of* the mantle like stuck-on marbles, with no socket, no lid, no integration. Cuttlefish have W-shaped pupils set into the mantle — these look like a snowman's coal. The head/mantle boundary is also missing entirely, so the eyes are floating on the dorsal surface instead of the lateral head.
- The arms in the 4×4 sheet are stiff dark rods, barely distinguishable from each other, all emerging from a single point. Real cuttlefish have 8 arms + 2 tentacles arranged in a crown around the mouth with visible separation and taper. Current rig looks like a fistful of dark spaghetti glued to the front. The mantleTaper sweep also barely changes the silhouette — rows look nearly identical, suggesting the taper parameter has too small an effective range or isn't actually driving geometry.

### Specific fixes
- Ship the chromatophore shader. Verify in `aquarium_after_v003.png` that the cuttlefish material is the chromatophore material, not a fallback `MeshStandardMaterial`. Log the active material at runtime. Then give it visible default params: 40–80 chromatophore spots, radius 0.02–0.04 of mantle length, pulse frequency 0.5–1.5 Hz, contrast 0.4. It should read immediately, not subtly.
- Redo the eyes. Sink them into the mantle (translate inward along -normal by ~0.6 * eyeRadius), add a shallow socket ring (torus or extruded ring), and give them a proper W-pupil texture or at minimum a horizontal slit pupil, not a plain sphere. Reference: cuttlefish eyes are lateral, not dorsal — move them down the side of the head.
- Rebuild the arm rig. 8 arms in a radial crown with per-arm phase offset for the curl animation, plus 2 longer tentacles that retract. Widen the mantleTaper sweep range (looks like 0.25–1.20 barely reads; try 0.15–2.00) and verify the parameter actually scales the spline radius, not just a cosmetic value.
- The 4×4 sheet should show dramatic silhouette variance across both axes. Currently row-to-row differences are near-invisible — either pick better parameter extremes or drop mantleTaper and sweep something with more visual payoff (armSpread? mantleLength?).

**All three tracks fail the 7/10 threshold.** None are ready to merge.

Priority order for the next iteration:
1. **Track B first.** The iridophore layer is actively degrading the scene — the baseline looks *better* than the after shot for the overall aquarium feel because the palette hasn't collapsed. Fix the material scoping and the composite math before anything else; Track A's eye issues are partially Track B bleed-through and may resolve for free.
2. **Track C second.** The chromatophore shader is the headline and it isn't visible. Ship the shader, redo the eyes, rebuild the arm crown. The current cuttlefish would embarrass the screensaver.
3. **Track A last.** Once B is fixed, re-evaluate — half the eye complaints likely stem from the skin shader swallowing the cornea. Then address view-space glint and per-fish eye scaling.

Do not accept any of these tracks in the current state. The baseline is more shippable than the after shot, which is the clearest possible signal that this iteration went backwards.
